# Phase 5.1 Context: Module 2 - Modern Banking

## Course Content Summary

Module 2 covers the History of Modern Banking with focus on:
1. Where modern banks came from (history)
2. What modern banks do (organization)
3. How modern banks earn profit (financial statements)

## Lessons Structure

### Lesson 2-0: Overview
- Business model concept
- Commercial vs Investment Banking distinction
- Module learning objectives

### Lesson 2-1.1: Early Banking in the United States
- Banking evolution (paper → digital)
- Traditional bank model (deposits → loans)
- Interest spread as profit source
- Three organizational forms: Unit Bank, Branch Network, Bank Holding Company
- Bank panics and Great Depression
- Glass-Steagall Act (1933): separation, FDIC, Regulation Q

### Lesson 2-1.2: Bank Holding Companies
- Why BHCs emerged (regulatory arbitrage)
- Golden Age of banking (1935-1980): 3-6-3 Rule
- Bank consolidation drivers: technology, non-bank competition
- Economies of scale and scope

### Lesson 2-1.3: Deregulation and Modern Banking
- Riegle-Neal Act (1994): interstate banking
- Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (1999): repeal Glass-Steagall
- Financial Holding Companies
- Fee income importance
- Too Big To Fail concept

## Key Concepts for Visualization

### Historical Timeline
- 1870-1985: Unit banking era (Illinois example)
- 1930s: Great Depression, bank failures
- 1933: Glass-Steagall Act
- 1935-1980: Golden Age (3-6-3 Rule)
- 1956: Bank Holding Company Act
- 1980s: Consolidation begins
- 1994: Riegle-Neal Act
- 1999: Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act
- 2008: Financial Crisis

### Banking Organization Evolution
1. Unit Bank → single location
2. Branch Network → geographic spread
3. Bank Holding Company → multiple banks under one owner
4. Financial Holding Company → banks + investment banks + insurance

### Interest Spread Model
```
Interest Income (from loans) - Interest Expense (to depositors) = Net Interest Income
Total Revenue = Net Interest Income + Fee Income
```

### Key Regulations Impact
- Glass-Steagall: Separation, stability, but fragmentation
- Riegle-Neal: Geographic freedom
- Gramm-Leach-Bliley: Product freedom
